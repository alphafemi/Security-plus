# Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)

## Overview

**Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)** is a broad term in cryptography referring to the policies, procedures, hardware, and software responsible for creating, distributing, managing, storing, revoking, and otherwise handling **digital certificates**.

Even in small organizations, PKI involves significant planning and many decisions around encryption methods. PKI is also commonly used to associate a certificate with a person or device — typically in conjunction with a **Certificate Authority (CA)** — to verify that a user or device is who they claim to be.

---

## Symmetric Encryption

**Symmetric encryption** means the same key is used for both encryption and decryption.

- Also called a **secret key algorithm** or **shared secret**
- Fast and low-overhead compared to asymmetric encryption
- The key must be shared with anyone who needs to decrypt the data

### Limitations

Sharing a single key with many parties creates a **scalability problem**. Managing which keys belong to which person or device becomes increasingly difficult beyond a small number of users.

> Despite this, symmetric encryption is still widely used today because of its speed and efficiency — often used alongside asymmetric encryption.

---

## Asymmetric Encryption

**Asymmetric encryption** uses two mathematically related keys — one for encryption and one for decryption — generated simultaneously as a **key pair**.

| Key | Access | Purpose |
|-----|--------|---------|
| **Private Key** | Owner only | Decrypts data encrypted with the public key |
| **Public Key** | Anyone | Encrypts data to send to the key owner |

### Key Properties

- You **cannot derive the private key** from the public key, even though they are mathematically related.
- Data encrypted with the public key **can only be decrypted** with the corresponding private key.
- The public key can be freely distributed (posted on websites, social media, etc.).
- The private key should be stored securely, typically protected with a password.

---

## How It Works: Alice and Bob Example

1. **Alice** generates a public/private key pair.
2. Alice distributes her **public key** openly and keeps her **private key** secure.
3. **Bob** writes a plaintext message: `"Hello, Alice."`
4. Bob encrypts it using Alice's public key, producing **ciphertext**.
5. Bob sends the ciphertext to Alice.
6. Alice decrypts the ciphertext using her **private key**, recovering the original plaintext.

Even if an interceptor obtains both the ciphertext and the public key, they **cannot decrypt the message** without the private key.

## Key Management

### Individual Use

When managing keys personally (e.g., with tools like **PGP** or **GPG**):

- Generate a key pair once at the start.
- Protect your private key with a strong password.
- Share your public key freely.

### Organizational Use

In environments with hundreds or thousands of users, formal key management strategies are required:

- **Third-party key management** — A trusted third party stores and manages private keys.
- **Key escrow** — The organization retains copies of private keys internally.

### Why Key Escrow Matters

Key escrow allows organizations to decrypt data when:

- A user **leaves the organization** or moves to a different department.
- A **government agency or partner** requires access to shared encrypted data.
- Maintaining **uptime and availability** of organizational data is critical.

> ⚠️ Handing private keys to a third party may seem controversial, but it is sometimes necessary for business continuity and compliance.

## Summary

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **PKI** | Framework for managing digital certificates and trust |
| **Symmetric Encryption** | Same key encrypts and decrypts; fast but hard to scale |
| **Asymmetric Encryption** | Key pair (public + private); more secure key distribution |
| **Certificate Authority (CA)** | Trusted entity that verifies identity via certificates |
| **Key Escrow** | Storing private keys with a trusted party for recovery |
