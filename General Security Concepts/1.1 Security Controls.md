## Overview

Security threats come from multiple directions, and organizations must implement comprehensive controls to protect:

- **Data** - Sensitive information and intellectual property
- **Systems** - Computing infrastructure and applications
- **Physical Assets** - Buildings, equipment, and facilities
- **People** - Employees and organizational stakeholders

This guide provides a framework for understanding different security control types and how they fit into four broad categories of implementation.

---

## Control Categories

Security controls fall into four primary categories based on how they are implemented:

### Technical Controls

Controls implemented using technology and systems. Examples include:
- Operating system policies and procedures
- Firewalls and network security
- Antivirus and anti-malware software
- Encryption and access management systems

**Who implements them:** IT security administrators and system engineers

### Managerial Controls

Controls implemented through policies, procedures, and organizational decision-making. Examples include:
- Security policies and documentation
- Compliance procedures
- Access control policies
- Incident response procedures

**Who implements them:** Security managers and policy creators

### Operational Controls

Controls implemented by people through daily activities and processes. Examples include:
- Security guard patrols
- Security awareness training and lunch-and-learns
- Employee onboarding processes
- Monitoring and alerting activities

**Who implements them:** Security personnel and operations staff

### Physical Controls

Controls that limit physical access to buildings, rooms, or devices. Examples include:
- Guard shacks and reception areas
- Fencing and door locks
- Badge readers and access systems
- Surveillance systems

**Who implements them:** Facilities and security personnel

## Control Types

### Preventive Controls

**Purpose:** Limit access to resources and prevent security events from occurring in the first place.

**Characteristics:**
- Stops unauthorized access before it happens
- The most proactive type of control
- Reduces the likelihood of a security incident

**Examples by Category:**

| Category | Example | Description |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Technical | Firewall rules | Prevent traffic from reaching unauthorized network segments |
| Managerial | Onboarding policies | Define access provisioning procedures for new employees |
| Operational | Guard shack ID checks | Verify identification before allowing entry to facility |
| Physical | Door locks | Physically prevent unauthorized access to rooms |

**Key Point:** Preventive controls are your first line of defense and should be implemented wherever possible.

---

### Deterrent Controls

**Purpose:** Discourage potential attackers by making them think twice about attempting an attack.

**Characteristics:**
- May not technically prevent access
- Psychological discouragement
- Creates consequences for unauthorized behavior
- Often visible or known to potential threats

**Examples by Category:**

| Category | Example | Description |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Technical | Application splash screens | Display security warnings and authorization requirements at startup |
| Managerial | Demotion/dismissal policies | Establish consequences for unauthorized data access |
| Operational | Reception desk greeting | Greet visitors and make them aware of monitoring |
| Physical | Warning signs | Inform people of consequences for unauthorized facility access |

**Key Point:** Deterrents work best when the potential attacker is aware of them and understands the consequences.

---

### Detective Controls

**Purpose:** Identify and warn of security breaches after they have occurred, enabling timely response.

**Characteristics:**
- Does not prevent the breach
- Provides early warning and documentation
- Enables rapid incident response
- Creates audit trails for forensic analysis

**Examples by Category:**

| Category | Example | Description |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Technical | System logs and log analysis | Collect and review system activity records for suspicious behavior |
| Managerial | Login report reviews | Review access logs daily or weekly to identify unauthorized access |
| Operational | Property patrols | Personnel patrol facilities to detect unauthorized entry or breaches |
| Physical | Motion detectors | Automatically notify security when motion is detected in restricted areas |

**Key Point:** Detective controls are critical for minimizing the damage caused by a successful attack by enabling quick detection and response.

---

### Corrective Controls

**Purpose:** Reverse or minimize the impact of a security event after it has been detected.

**Characteristics:**
- Implemented after a breach is discovered
- May reverse the impact completely
- Allows business to continue with minimal disruption
- Focuses on restoration and recovery

**Examples by Category:**

| Category | Example | Description |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Technical | System restore from backup | Erase infected system and restore from known-good backup |
| Managerial | Incident reporting policies | Create procedures for rolling security issues into alerts and notifications |
| Operational | Law enforcement contact | Contact authorities to address and correct physical security breaches |
| Physical | Fire extinguisher | Contain and extinguish fire to prevent further damage |

**Key Point:** Corrective controls are your recovery mechanism; backups and disaster recovery procedures are essential components.

---

### Compensating Controls

**Purpose:** Provide alternative protection when primary controls cannot be fully implemented.

**Characteristics:**
- Used when the ideal control is unavailable
- Often temporary until permanent solution is deployed
- Uses alternative means to achieve the same security goal
- Reduces risk to an acceptable level

**Examples by Category:**

| Category | Example | Description |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Technical | Firewall rule (temporary) | Block traffic to vulnerable application while awaiting security patch |
| Managerial | Separation of duties | Divide responsibilities among multiple people to limit any individual's access |
| Operational | Multiple security staff | Require simultaneous presence of multiple guards to prevent unauthorized access |
| Physical | Backup generator | Provide alternative power while main power is being restored |

**Key Point:** Compensating controls bridge the gap between vulnerability discovery and permanent remediation.

---

### Directive Controls

**Purpose:** Direct people to perform actions that increase security.

**Characteristics:**
- Relies on human compliance and decision-making
- The weakest type of control
- Requires user understanding and judgment
- Effectiveness depends on awareness and training

**Examples by Category:**

| Category | Example | Description |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Technical | Encrypted folder policy | Direct users to store sensitive data in protected folders |
| Managerial | Compliance policies | Establish procedures and processes for security in the organization |
| Operational | Security training courses | Educate employees on proper security policies and best practices |
| Physical | "Authorized Personnel Only" signs | Direct people to respect access boundaries (may lack physical locks) |

**Key Point:** Directive controls should be supported by technical, operational, and physical controls to be effective.

## Implementation Considerations

### Layered Defense

A single control is rarely sufficient. Implement controls across multiple categories and types:

1. **Prevention Layer** - Firewall rules, access controls, physical barriers
2. **Detection Layer** - Logging, monitoring, alarms
3. **Response Layer** - Incident procedures, backup systems
4. **Recovery Layer** - Disaster recovery, business continuity

### Customization by Organization

The specific controls you implement will depend on:

- Your organization's risk profile
- Industry regulations and compliance requirements
- Available resources and budget
- Threat landscape and identified vulnerabilities
- Business criticality of assets

### Evolving Threats

As technology changes and security processes evolve:

- New control types and categories may emerge
- Existing controls may become less effective
- New threats will require new protective measures
- Regular security reviews and updates are essential

### Testing Your Controls

Validate your controls by:

1. **Testing Preventive Controls** - Attempt to access protected resources
2. **Verifying Detective Controls** - Confirm alerting and logging work as intended
3. **Exercising Corrective Controls** - Test backup restoration and incident response
4. **Reviewing Policies** - Ensure managerial controls are current and relevant
5. **Conducting Drills** - Practice operational responses to security incidents

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Multiple Control Categories** - Use technical, managerial, operational, and physical controls together
2. **Multiple Control Types** - Combine preventive, detective, corrective, and other types for defense in depth
3. **Balanced Approach** - Don't rely solely on any single control or category
4. **Continuous Improvement** - Regularly review and update your security controls
5. **Customization** - Tailor your controls to your organization's specific needs and risks
