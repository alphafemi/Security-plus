# Phishing

## Overview

**Phishing** is a form of social engineering that uses deceptive communications — email, text, voice calls, or other channels — to trick targets into revealing private information such as usernames, passwords, or financial details. The attacker impersonates a trusted entity to manufacture a false sense of legitimacy.

---

## How to Spot a Phishing Attempt

Phishing messages typically contain one or more of the following red flags:

- **Unexpected urgency** — deadlines like "your account will be blocked" pressure the target to act without thinking
- **Mismatched sender address** — the sender's domain doesn't match the organization being impersonated (e.g., an Apple iCloud address claiming to be from Rackspace)
- **Typosquatted domains** — links point to domains with subtle misspellings (e.g., `professormessor.com` instead of `professormesser.com`)
- **Visual inconsistencies** — font irregularities, poor spacing, or slightly off branding on fake login pages
- **Generic salutations** — "Dear user" rather than your actual name
- **Unsolicited requests** — asking you to confirm, verify, or update information you never requested to change

> **Best practice:** Never click links directly from an email. Navigate to the site manually by typing the address into your browser.

---

## Common Phishing Techniques

### Typosquatting

Attackers register domain names that are close misspellings of legitimate ones, betting that a distracted user won't notice the difference.

**Example:**
| Legitimate | Typosquatted |
|-----------|-------------|
| `professormesser.com` | `professormessor.com` |
| `paypal.com` | `paypa1.com` |

### Pretexting

The attacker fabricates a believable scenario (a "pretext") to justify their request. The story is designed to create urgency or concern — drawing the target into the drama before they have time to think critically.

**Example:** A caller claims to be from Visa, says there's a problem with an automated payment, and asks you to confirm your card details over the phone. There is no problem; the entire scenario is invented.

### Email Spoofing

Attackers may forge the sender address to appear as though the message is coming from a trusted source — a colleague, a bank, or a known service provider. Even if the display name looks correct, the underlying email address or domain often reveals the deception on closer inspection.

## Phishing by Channel

### Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Phishing conducted over the phone. Attackers may spoof a caller ID to display a bank or government number, then impersonate a representative to extract account credentials, card numbers, or personal information.

**Common pretexts:**
- Fraudulent charges on your account
- Problems with an automated payment
- Account verification or security checks

### Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Phishing delivered via text message. SMS messages feel immediate and personal, making them effective at prompting quick, unthinking responses.

**Common pretexts:**
- Package delivery failures (impersonating USPS, FedEx, UPS)
- Bank alerts or suspicious activity warnings
- Prize claims or account verification requests

**Example message:**
> "USPS: Your package has been suspended due to an incorrect delivery address. Click here to reschedule: [link]"

---

## What Attackers Do With Access

Gaining access to an email account, for example, opens far more than just that inbox:

- **Password resets** — attackers use "forgot password" flows on other services (PayPal, banking apps) to have reset links sent to the compromised inbox
- **Financial reconnaissance** — scanning emails for account numbers, invoices, or login credentials stored in messages
- **Lateral phishing** — sending phishing messages to the victim's contacts using their trusted identity
- **Malware delivery** — links in phishing messages may download malware rather than capturing credentials directly

## Other Common Phishing Scams

| Scam | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Fake check scam** | Victim receives a fraudulent check and is asked to wire back a portion of the funds |
| **Phone verification code scam** | Attacker asks the victim to share a one-time code that was just sent to their phone, gaining access to an account |
| **Crypto scam** | Fake investment opportunity or giveaway designed to steal existing wallet credentials or funds |
| **Invoice fraud** | Fake invoice sent via email requesting payment for a service never rendered |

## Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Phishing** | Deceptive communications designed to steal credentials or personal information |
| **Vishing** | Voice-based phishing via phone calls |
| **Smishing** | SMS-based phishing via text messages |
| **Typosquatting** | Registering misspelled domains to impersonate legitimate sites |
| **Pretexting** | Fabricating a scenario to manipulate the target into complying |
| **Email spoofing** | Forging a sender address to appear as a trusted source |
