# Impersonation

## Overview

**Impersonation** is when an attacker pretends to be someone they are not in order to gain trust, extract information, or take action on behalf of their target. It is one of the most common social engineering techniques and underpins many phishing, vishing, and fraud attacks.

---

## How Impersonation Works

Attackers use impersonation to lower a target's guard. By presenting themselves as a familiar, authoritative, or trustworthy figure, they make the target more likely to comply with requests they would otherwise refuse.

### Common Personas

| Persona | Why It Works |
|---------|-------------|
| **IT Help Desk** | Employees trust internal support staff and are used to receiving calls about system issues |
| **Company Executive** | Authority and rank create pressure to comply without questioning |
| **Government Agency** | Official language and implied legal consequences induce fear and urgency |
| **Bank or Credit Card Company** | Financial urgency triggers immediate concern |
| **Tech Company (e.g., Microsoft)** | Broad name recognition and fear of computer problems lower skepticism |

### Real-World Examples

> *"Hello, sir. My name is Wendy. And I'm from Microsoft Windows. This is an urgent checkup call for your computer as we have found several problems with it."*

> *"This is an enforcement action executed by the US Treasury intending your serious attention."*

> *"Congratulations on your excellent payment history. You now qualify for 0% interest rates on all of your credit card accounts."*

Each of these calls uses a recognizable name or authority to manufacture instant credibility.

---

## Tactics Used in Impersonation

### Information Elicitation

The attacker's goal is to extract sensitive information — but they cannot simply ask for it directly. Instead, they construct a convincing story that makes the request seem natural or necessary.

**Example:** Rather than asking "What is your bank account number?", an attacker might say there has been a problem sending a payment to a third party on your behalf, and they need your account details to resolve it.

Information commonly targeted:
- Usernames and passwords
- Credit card numbers
- Social Security numbers
- Bank account details
- Date of birth or home address

### Technical Jargon

Flooding the target with technical terms shifts their focus from questioning the legitimacy of the call to trying to understand the content. While the target is processing unfamiliar language, their skepticism drops.

### Authority and Rank

Claiming to be a VP, director, or government official creates implicit pressure. Most people are reluctant to challenge or question someone who outranks them — even a stranger on the phone.

## Identity Fraud

A natural extension of impersonation, **identity fraud** occurs when an attacker uses a victim's personal information to act *as* that person — typically for financial gain.

| Fraud Type | How It Works |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Credit card fraud** | Attacker opens a card in the victim's name, uses their address, and makes purchases with no intent to pay |
| **Bank account fraud** | Attacker opens an account under the victim's name to store illicit funds or access loans |
| **Loan fraud** | Using the victim's identity as an established bank customer to take out loans |
| **Tax fraud** | Attacker files the victim's tax return early and redirects the refund to their own account |
| **Benefits fraud** | Attacker claims government benefits under the victim's identity |

All of these activities appear on the victim's financial and legal record — sometimes going undetected for months or years.

## Prevention

### Do Not Volunteer Information

Legitimate support staff, banks, and government agencies will not ask for passwords, full card numbers, or Social Security numbers over the phone. If someone is asking — regardless of who they claim to be — treat the request with suspicion.

> **Remember:** Your IT help desk does not need your password to fix a problem on your system.

### Protect Personal Details

Never provide the following over phone, email, or messaging unless you initiated the contact and have independently verified the recipient:

- Home address
- Social Security number
- Date of birth
- Bank account or routing numbers
- Passwords or PINs

### Verify Independently

If a caller seems legitimate but is requesting sensitive information, ask if you can call them back — then look up the organization's public phone number yourself and call through that. Do not use a number provided by the caller.

### Make Verification a Standard Process

Organizations should establish clear policies that no sensitive information is shared over the phone without a verification step — regardless of who is asking or what rank they claim to hold. This normalizes the process so employees do not feel awkward applying it.

## Summary

| Concept | Key Takeaway |
|---------|-------------|
| **Impersonation** | Pretending to be a trusted person or organization to manipulate a target |
| **Elicitation** | Extracting sensitive information through a crafted story rather than a direct ask |
| **Identity fraud** | Using a victim's personal details to open accounts, take loans, or claim benefits |
| **Authority impersonation** | Claiming executive or government rank to suppress skepticism and pressure compliance |
| **Prevention** | Never volunteer information; verify callers independently through public contact details |
