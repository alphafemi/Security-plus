# Threat Actors

## Overview

A **threat actor** is any entity whose actions negatively affect the security of others. Understanding who an attacker is — and why they are attacking — helps organizations tailor their defenses appropriately.

Threat actors are characterized by four key attributes:

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Origin** | Internal (inside the organization) or external |
| **Resources** | Financial backing and access to tools and infrastructure |
| **Sophistication** | Technical skill level, from script execution to custom exploit development |
| **Motivation** | The goal driving the attack — financial gain, disruption, ideology, etc. |

---

## Threat Actor Types

### Nation State

**Origin:** External  
**Resources:** Extensive  
**Sophistication:** Very high  
**Motivations:** Data exfiltration, political disruption, espionage, sabotage, acts of war

Nation-state actors operate as arms of a government, with the full financial and technical resources of that government behind them. They are capable of sustaining long-running, simultaneous attacks against multiple targets.

These attacks are commonly referred to as **Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)** — highly sophisticated, well-funded campaigns that can persist undetected for extended periods.

**Common targets:** Military systems, utilities, financial infrastructure, government networks

> **Example:** The **Stuxnet** worm, attributed to the United States and Israel, was specifically engineered to physically destroy Iranian nuclear centrifuges — one of the most sophisticated cyberattacks ever documented.

---

### Unskilled Attacker

**Origin:** Primarily external, occasionally internal  
**Resources:** Very limited  
**Sophistication:** Low  
**Motivations:** Disruption, data exfiltration, philosophical or political reasons

Unskilled attackers run pre-written scripts without a deep understanding of how they work or why they may fail. If a script succeeds, the attack succeeds. If it fails, the attacker lacks the knowledge to troubleshoot or adapt.

Because they lack resources and sophistication, unskilled attackers typically look for the path of least resistance — exploiting well-known vulnerabilities using publicly available tools.

---

### Hacktivist

**Origin:** Primarily external  
**Resources:** Limited (some groups fundraise)  
**Sophistication:** Often high  
**Motivations:** Political ideology, philosophical beliefs, public exposure of wrongdoing, disruption

A **hacktivist** (hacker + activist) uses technical skills to advance a cause or political agenda. Despite limited funding, hacktivists can be technically sophisticated and targeted in their methods.

**Common attack types:**
- Denial of service (taking down websites or services)
- Website defacement (replacing content with their own messaging)
- Data exfiltration and public release of sensitive documents

---

### Insider Threat

**Origin:** Internal  
**Resources:** Existing organizational access  
**Sophistication:** Medium — compensated by intimate knowledge of the environment  
**Motivations:** Revenge, financial gain, coercion

Insider threats are among the most difficult to detect and stop. The attacker already has legitimate access to systems and data, knows where sensitive information is stored, and understands how to navigate or bypass existing security controls.

Proper **vetting during the hiring process** is one of the most effective defenses against insider threats, as prevention is far easier than detection after the fact.

---

### Organized Crime

**Origin:** External  
**Resources:** Significant — often structured like a business  
**Sophistication:** High  
**Motivations:** Financial profit

Organized cybercrime groups operate with a corporate-like structure, where different members specialize in distinct roles:

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| **Hacker** | Conducts the intrusion |
| **Exploit developer** | Creates and maintains attack tools |
| **Data broker** | Sells exfiltrated data to buyers |
| **Customer support** | Manages ransom negotiations with victims |

Ransomware operations are a common output of organized crime groups, complete with payment portals and negotiation infrastructure.

---

### Shadow IT

**Origin:** Internal  
**Resources:** Limited to departmental budgets or personal credit cards  
**Sophistication:** Low to moderate (often no formal IT background)  
**Motivations:** Convenience, bypassing IT constraints

**Shadow IT** refers to groups or departments within an organization that build their own infrastructure and use their own tools without the knowledge or approval of the IT department. They operate outside of standard controls such as change management, security reviews, and backup policies.

Common shadow IT approaches include:
- Subscribing to cloud services via personal or departmental credit cards
- Installing unauthorized applications on work devices
- Building workflows that handle sensitive data without security oversight

> **Risk:** Shadow IT personnel often have no background in security, meaning sensitive data may be stored, transmitted, or processed without any of the protections that IT would normally require.

## Threat Actor Comparison

| Threat Actor | Origin | Resources | Sophistication | Common Motivations |
|-------------|--------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Nation State** | External | Extensive | Very high | Espionage, disruption, warfare |
| **Unskilled Attacker** | External | Very limited | Low | Disruption, notoriety |
| **Hacktivist** | External | Limited | High | Ideology, political activism |
| **Insider Threat** | Internal | Existing access | Medium | Revenge, financial gain |
| **Organized Crime** | External | Significant | High | Financial profit |
| **Shadow IT** | Internal | Limited | Low | Convenience, autonomy |

---

## Why Motivation Matters

Identifying the motivation behind an attack helps security teams prioritize defenses and respond appropriately:

- A **nation-state** attacker suggests a need for hardened infrastructure, network segmentation, and APT detection capabilities
- An **insider threat** suggests stronger access controls, least-privilege policies, and user behavior monitoring
- **Organized crime** suggests prioritizing ransomware protection, offsite backups, and endpoint detection
- **Shadow IT** suggests a need for better internal governance, approved tooling alternatives, and user education

Understanding the *who* and *why* behind a threat is just as important as understanding the technical mechanics of the attack itself.

## Summary

| Concept | Key Takeaway |
|---------|-------------|
| **Threat actor** | Any entity whose actions negatively impact others' security |
| **APT** | Advanced Persistent Threat — long-running, well-funded attacks typically associated with nation states |
| **Nation state** | Most resourced and sophisticated threat actor; government-backed |
| **Unskilled attacker** | Runs existing scripts without deep understanding; targets easy vulnerabilities |
| **Hacktivist** | Ideologically motivated; targets reputation and public exposure |
| **Insider threat** | Hardest to detect; leverages existing legitimate access |
| **Organized crime** | Profit-driven; operates like a business with specialized roles |
| **Shadow IT** | Internal risk from unsanctioned tools and infrastructure built outside IT oversight |
